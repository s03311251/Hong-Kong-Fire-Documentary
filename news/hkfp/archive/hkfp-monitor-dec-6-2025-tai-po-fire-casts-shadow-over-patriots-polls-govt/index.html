As thousands show up to mourn victims of the Tai Po fire, candidates running in this Sunday’s Legislative Council (LegCo) elections are gearing up for a subdued race.

Welcome to HKFP Monitor: Your weekly bird’s-eye view of the latest news and trends across Hong Kong’s media landscape. Subscribe to receive our free, essential round-ups by email every Friday.

Welcome back to HKFP Monitor. This week, Hong Kong continues to reel from the fatal Wang Fuk Court blaze in Tai Po, with the death toll reaching at least 159. As thousands show up to mourn victims at a makeshift altar near the fire site, candidates running in this Sunday’s Legislative Council (LegCo) elections are gearing up for a subdued race.

Meanwhile, a podium at a nearby residential estate, which was occupied last week by hundreds of Hongkongers mobilising to provide supplies for the fire victims, has been cleared out, replaced by a police tent and patrolling officers. It’s a visual depiction of the government’s attempt to write the narrative in its own words – a dynamic also playing out through silenced social media commentators and sedition arrests.

| THE VOTE MUST GO ON

The fatal Tai Po fire, which broke out on November 26, has cast a shadow over the “patriots only” LegCo polls on Sunday. A government-organised concert to promote voting was cancelled, election forums began with a moment of silence, and one resident told local media he would “definitely not vote.”

Campaigning has been slow to pick up since Chief Executive John Lee confirmed on Tuesday that the elections would go ahead as scheduled, as candidates – like everyone else – attempt to navigate these unprecedented times.

‘Patriots only’: Since Beijing’s electoral overhaul in 2021, which effectively shut out opposition candidates and reduced the number of publicly elected seats, elections have not attracted as high a level of participation as before. The LegCo elections in 2021 and the District Council elections in 2023 saw record-low turnouts, as people saw the elections as predetermined and the candidates no longer able to represent them.

A muted affair: Now, it looks like the December 7 elections will be even more muted than anticipated. By Friday morning, few candidates had returned to the streets to campaign.

According to local media, there were campaign volunteers passing out flyers on the streets, and many were no longer blaring candidate speeches from loudspeakers like they used to.

Signs of election life were more apparent only on their Facebook pages. Candidates changed their profile pictures from monochrome images – many of them showing their party’s logos, others displaying flowers, candles or other signs of mourning – back to their campaign headshots. Some posted clips of their speeches at the election forums, while others shared videos of fellow politicians endorsing them.

Inquiry commission vs review committee: On Tuesday, Lee announced that the government would set up an independent review committee to probe the fire. While major political parties lauded the move, others pointed out that a review committee is not the same as a commission of inquiry. The latter, established under the Commissions of Inquiry Ordinance, comes with the legal power to summon witnesses – something that a review committee lacks.

During the Law Society of Hong Kong’s forum for the legal sector race, the two candidates, Virginia Lee and Nicholas Chan, both said they supported setting up a review committee.

Chan acknowledged that a review committee differed from a commission of inquiry, which can subpoena anyone.

“But the chief executive has explained himself. And I agree,” Chan said in Cantonese.

On Thursday, HKFP emailed all election candidates running in geographical constituencies to ask whether they supported the establishment of an inquiry commission. Only one, Jeremy Young, who is running in the Central and Western District as a Liberal Party representative, replied.

“I think the [chief executive] has already mentioned his intentions. I will monitor whether the current plan is effective before answering this question,” Young wrote.

| ARRESTS & SILENCED VOICES

Increasingly, news is being consumed not only through media outlets but also through individual personalities on social media. This trend became clear when the Tai Po fire broke out: Social media posts from two university students – Ellie Yuen and Hailey Cheng – went viral.

Yuen, a journalism student at the University of Hong Kong, published an Instagram carousel post hours after the blaze started, shining an early spotlight on fire-retardant mesh and pro-Beijing district councillor Peggy Wong, who is rumoured to have played a role in the construction project.

Meanwhile, Cheng – a City University math student and the whistleblower in a scandal involving an AI app claimed to have been built by a secondary school pupil – was an active presence on Threads and X. She commented, in both Chinese and English, on developments relating to the fire, including the government’s announcement that it would expedite the phasing out of bamboo scaffolding and set up an independent review committee instead of a commission of inquiry.

Silenced: But this week, both of them wrote on social media that going forward, they would not be posting about the fire again.

In a Threads post published in the early hours of Thursday, Yuen said she had “concluded all news-related work regarding the HK fire and will not provide any further comments, additions, or related work, for obvious reasons.”

Hours earlier on Wednesday evening, Cheng said on Threads that she had received “very accurate tip-offs” and would likely not be making any comments about the fire. Later, on Threads and X, she wrote that she would not have any association with any “foreign elements” that “undermine peace in society,” and that she had severed all ties that could be misunderstood as such.

Arrests and a cancelled press conference: Yuen’s and Cheng’s silence follows developments that some say point to the government’s attempt to snuff out narratives that may not align with its own.

On Tuesday, a civil society-led press conference that was to discuss building maintenance issues in light of the Tai Po fire was called off at the last minute. Hours before the press conference, one of the speakers, Bruce Liu, solicitor and former chair of the pro-democracy group Association for Democracy and People’s Livelihood (ADPL), was taken in by national security police.

On Saturday last week, Chinese University student Miles Kwan was arrested on suspicion of a sedition offence after creating a petition demanding the government respond to “four demands” in relation to the fire. The next day, former district councillor Kenneth Cheung and a volunteer handling supplies for fire victims were also arrested for alleged sedition.

‘Slanderous remarks’: The Hong Kong government issued a statement late on Wednesday, warning of “foreign forces, including anti-China media organisations, anti-China and destabilising forces” making “unfounded and slanderous remarks” about the authorities’ fire relief work.

Some netizens say the Hong Kong government’s response follows the playbook of official disaster responses across the border, where mainland authorities heavily police online comments and bring in people deemed troublemakers “for tea.”

A recent example is an apartment fire in Urumqi, the capital of Xinjiang’s region, on November 24, 2022. Ten people died, and the casualties were partially blamed on China’s harsh zero-Covid measures, which prevented residents from fleeing the inferno. The blaze sparked the “white paper protests” – protesters held up blank sheets of paper – a reference to their silenced voices and the lack of freedom of speech.

A crackdown quickly followed, and in the months afterwards, scores more people were apprehended.

‘The truth’: In Hong Kong, there have been no such protest scenes. A grassroots effort, however, did materialise at a podium near the fire site, where volunteers distributed supplies and offered counselling, physiotherapy, and other services to victims.

For the first two days after the fire broke out on November 26, the podium at nearby Kwong Fuk Estate was the closest thing to an organic mass gathering the city had seen in years, resembling a buzzing market. By Saturday, however, police had cleared out all volunteers and set up patrol tents.

At a rest area nearby, mourners have been lining up to leave flowers at a pavilion converted into a makeshift altar. Volunteers are handing out pens and paper for people to leave messages for the victims and write their thoughts. Many of the messages allude to wishing to find “the truth” so that victims can rest in peace.

Police have reportedly asked volunteers to remove “sensitive” notes, including those saying: “Add oil Hong Kong.”

| LOCAL MEDIA MONITOR

Legal scholar Johannes Chan has ceased penning opinion pieces for local newspaper Ming Pao, where he has served as a columnist for almost 23 years.

Chan, a former dean of the University of Hong Kong’s law faculty, has seen his columns criticised by authorities before. Earlier this year, Secretary for Security Chris Tang condemned Chan for “undermining the rule of law” in an opinion piece about a court ruling that found lawmaker Lam Cheuk-ting guilty of rioting during the protests in 2019.

In his final piece published on Wednesday, Chan said he “knew this day would eventually come.”

“Being able to write until today is actually already rare and admirable,” he said.

He added that different people had varying opinions about his writing and that this was understandable.

Ming Pao is one of the few newspapers that still host op-eds by more liberal writers. Two years ago, it axed a cartoon strip by artist Zunzi following complaints from the government.

| STATE MEDIA MONITOR

It is not unheard of for local media outlets to redact a report because it was deemed “sensitive” by higher-ups. Case in point: iCable, which deleted a news segment last month. In the report, its journalist interviewed people outside an election forum who appeared clueless about who the candidates were – suggesting they were not legitimate supporters.

Such things hardly ever happen to tightly controlled state-backed media – and yet, it happened twice to pro-Beijing newspaper Ta Kung Pao in the past week.

On November 28, the newspaper published a one-page investigative report, headlined “Unveiling the Dark Secrets Behind Scaffolding Repairs.” The article, which reported on corruption and bid-rigging in construction projects, was swiftly taken down by the next day.

It appeared to be the first piece in an investigative series.

On Ta Kung Pao’s website, where readers can access an archive of past editions, page 7, where the report appeared, has vanished entirely. The URL for the digital article has also been removed.

Days later, on Wednesday, Ta Kung Pao published an op-ed by pro-Beijing commentator Chris Wat about the deep-rooted problems in governance that might have contributed to the fire. According to HK Propaganda, an Instagram page that tracks state media, the op-ed was revised seven times before being taken down.

Among the changes before the deletion was editing a sentence that read “‘from stability to prosperity’ is not a slogan but a thorough change that shakes up the bureaucratic system and prevents future tragedies” to remove the reference to a bureaucratic shake-up.

| SOCIAL MEDIA MONITOR

Space to grieve: An Instagram page called Wang Fuk Memories has been created to serve as a visual archive of the housing estate before the massive fire. The page invites residents to submit photos, aiming to provide a “space to grieve and remember.”

Wang Fuk Court has become a household name in Hong Kong now, with images of the blocks on fire, and the scorched towers in the aftermath, dominating news reports and social media for days. But perhaps few know what the taupe-coloured estate looked like before the blaze.

“By collecting and sharing old photos and related stories of the housing estate, home to over 4,000 residents, we hope to rebuild cherished memories for families who lost not only their homes but also irreplaceable photos and keepsakes in the tragedy,” a post read.

Battle suit: Residents of Wang Chi House, the only building in the eight-block Wang Fuk Court unaffected by the fire, began returning to their homes on Wednesday to retrieve their belongings.

As news reports focused on residents gathering their clothes, identity documents, and other necessities, Gary Chan – a lawmaker from the Democratic Alliance for the Betterment and Progress of Hong Kong (DAB) – had a different fixation.

Chan, who is running in the upcoming legislative elections, shared a WhatsApp message from a Wang Chi House resident who sent him a picture of a DAB vest for Chan’s volunteers. The sender wrote that while picking up items from his home, this “battle suit couldn’t be forgotten.”

The lawmaker’s post on Wednesday attracted almost 300 angry reactions, more than half the total reactions received. Comments were limited on his post.

| HKFP PHOTO OF THE WEEK

Thousands of Hongkongers turned up at Tai Po to mourn the at least 159 who lost their lives in the blaze. Many hugged their loved ones as they observed a moment of silence.
